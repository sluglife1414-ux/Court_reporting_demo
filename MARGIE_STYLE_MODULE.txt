MASTER PRINCIPLE (Margie's Core Teaching):
   Putting commas around an element simply means, at the most basic level,
   that it could be removed from the sentence and that there would still
   be a sentence. A pair of commas does NOT mean the element is
   "nonessential" or a "parenthetical" — those terms might apply, but
   they are not the ONLY reason to surround something with commas.

================================================================================
SECTION 1 — COMMAS: DIRECT ADDRESS
================================================================================

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
MARGIE RULE [M-001] | Commas — Direct Address | [CR-CRITICAL]
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
RULE: Always surround a name or title used in direct address with commas.
      The term of address can appear at the beginning, middle, or end of
      the sentence — it always gets commas around it.

CORRECT:   I have already sent it, sir.
CORRECT:   I have already sent it, sir, to all of the people that are involved.
CORRECT:   Your Honor, I am asking that you grant us a continuance.
CORRECT:   I am asking that you grant us a continuance, Your Honor.
CORRECT:   I am asking that you grant us a continuance, Your Honor, so that
              I might investigate this new evidence.
CORRECT:   Show that to us, Counsel, so that I am sure of what you are saying.

NOTE: Direct address terms include: sir, ma'am, Counsel, Your Honor, Judge,
      Doctor, and any name used when speaking directly to a person.

SECTION 2 — COMMAS: CITY/STATE AND FULL DATES
================================================================================

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
MARGIE RULE [M-002] | Commas — City/State | [CR-CRITICAL]
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
RULE: When a state name follows a city name, put a comma before AND after
      the state. The state is a removable element; the comma after it is
      required whenever the sentence continues.

CORRECT:   We were on our way to Provo, Utah.
CORRECT:   We were on our way to Provo, Utah, to visit family.
CORRECT:   It took place in Springfield, Illinois, and not in Springfield,
              Ohio, on the 15th of last month.

INCORRECT: We were on our way to Provo, Utah to visit family.
              [Missing the closing comma after Utah]

NOTE: Same principle applies to country names following city names.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
MARGIE RULE [M-003] | Commas — Full Dates (Month Day, Year) | [CR-CRITICAL]
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
RULE: When a full date (month, day, year) appears mid-sentence, put a
      comma before AND after the year. The year is a removable element.

CORRECT:   It is dated September 22, 2014, and was mailed that very day.
CORRECT:   The information was in the July 19, 2012, letter and not in the
              July 19, 2013, letter.

INCORRECT: It is dated September 22, 2014 and was mailed that very day.
              [Missing the closing comma after 2014]

SECTION 3 — COMMAS: DATES (MONTH + YEAR — NO COMMA)
================================================================================

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
MARGIE RULE [M-004] | Commas — Month + Year Dates | [CR-CRITICAL] [OVERRIDES GREGG]
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
RULE: When only a month and year appear (no specific day), do NOT put a
      comma between the month and the year.

CORRECT:   We moved there in June 2012.
CORRECT:   September 2004 is the year the twins were born.

INCORRECT: We moved there in June, 2012.
              [No comma between month and year when no day is given]
INCORRECT: September, 2004, is the year the twins were born.
              [No comma between month and year]

NOTE: [OVERRIDES GREGG] — Some standard style guides permit the comma
      between month and year. Margie's rule: NO comma. This is the
      professional court reporting standard.

SECTION 4 — COMMAS: INTERRUPTERS AND PARENTHETICAL ELEMENTS
================================================================================

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
MARGIE RULE [M-005] | Commas — Interrupters (Mid-Sentence Elements) | [CR-CRITICAL]
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
RULE: Any element that interrupts the flow of a sentence and could be
      removed without destroying the sentence gets commas around it
      (one comma before, one after).

CORRECT:   He was derelict, in my judgment, in fulfilling his duty.
CORRECT:   She was interested, I believe, in the truth.

INCORRECT: He was derelict in my judgment, in fulfilling his duty.
              [Missing the opening comma before "in my judgment"]

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
MARGIE RULE [M-006] | Commas — "However" as Interrupter | [CR-CRITICAL]
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
RULE: "However" can appear at the beginning, middle, or end of a sentence.
      Wherever it appears, surround it with commas (or use a comma after
      it at the start of a sentence).

CORRECT:   However, I had spoken with him about the incident.
              [Beginning of sentence: comma after]
CORRECT:   I had spoken with him about the incident, however.
              [End of sentence: comma before]
CORRECT:   I had spoken with him about the incident, however, before I
              knew the full story.
              [Middle of sentence: commas around it]

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
MARGIE RULE [M-007] | Commas — Movable Modifying Phrases | [CR-CRITICAL]
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
RULE: When a phrase can be moved within the sentence OR removed entirely,
      it must be set off with commas. The key test: can you remove it and
      still have a complete, sensible sentence?

EXAMPLES — No commas needed (phrase is essential, not movable in meaning):
CORRECT:   He walked somewhat gingerly on his sore leg.
CORRECT:   He walked later in the evening to the liquor store to buy a bottle.

EXAMPLES — Commas needed (phrase is a true interrupter):
CORRECT:   He walked, somewhat gingerly, on his sore leg.
              [When "somewhat gingerly" is treated as a removable aside]
CORRECT:   He walked, later in the evening, to the liquor store to buy a bottle.
              [When "later in the evening" is treated as a removable aside]

INCORRECT: He walked somewhat gingerly, on his sore leg.
              [Single comma splits the phrase from its object — wrong]
INCORRECT: He walked later in the evening, to the liquor store to buy a bottle.
              [Single comma creates a half-pair — wrong]

NOTE: Either use NO commas (phrase reads naturally as part of the sentence)
      or use a PAIR of commas (phrase is a true removable interrupter).
      A lone comma that splits a phrase from its object is always wrong.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
MARGIE RULE [M-008] | Commas — "In case you don't know" and Similar Asides | [CR-CRITICAL]
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
RULE: Parenthetical clauses like "in case you don't know" must be surrounded
      with a pair of commas when they interrupt the sentence.

CORRECT:   They were involved, in case you don't know, in another accident.

INCORRECT: They were involved in case you don't know, in another accident.
              [Missing opening comma]

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
MARGIE RULE [M-009] | Commas — "Nevertheless" and Similar Transitional Words | [CR-CRITICAL]
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
RULE: "Nevertheless" and similar transitional words used as interrupters
      must be surrounded by a pair of commas. Do not use a single comma
      that only follows the word.

CORRECT:   Are you saying, nevertheless, that you intend to file that report?

INCORRECT: Are you saying nevertheless, that you intend to file that report?
              [Single comma after "nevertheless" — wrong; creates a half-pair]

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
MARGIE RULE [M-010] | Commas — Introductory Dependent Clauses | [CR-CRITICAL]
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
RULE: A dependent clause that begins a sentence is followed by a comma.
      The same clause can also appear mid-sentence as an interrupter,
      in which case it takes a pair of commas around it.

CORRECT:   When I arrived that night, I did not have the chance to speak
              with him.
              [Clause at the beginning: comma after it]
CORRECT:   I did not have the chance, when I arrived that night, to speak
              with him.
              [Clause in the middle: pair of commas]
CORRECT:   I did not have the chance to speak with him when I arrived
              that night.
              [Clause at the end: no comma needed]

CORRECT:   At the time of the incident we are discussing, she was unemployed.
CORRECT:   She was, at the time of the incident we are discussing, unemployed.
CORRECT:   She was unemployed at the time of the incident we are discussing.

SECTION 5 — COMMAS: WORDS LIKE "NOW," "THEREFORE," "OBVIOUSLY," "AGAIN"
================================================================================

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
MARGIE RULE [M-011] | Commas — "Now" | [CR-CRITICAL]
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
RULE: "Now" used as a transitional/parenthetical word at the beginning of
      a sentence is followed by a comma. If it is simply an adverb
      modifying the verb (meaning "at this moment"), no comma is used.

CORRECT:   Now, I want to talk about the 10th.
              ["Now" = transitional, meaning "moving on to..." — comma after]
CORRECT:   Now I want to talk about the 10th.
              ["Now" = at this time — no comma (also acceptable per context)]

NOTE: In court reporting, "Now" before a question almost always functions
      as a transitional word and takes a comma. Read the speaker's intent.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
MARGIE RULE [M-012] | Commas — "Therefore" | [CR-CRITICAL]
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
RULE: "Therefore" used as an interrupter mid-sentence must be surrounded
      by a pair of commas.

CORRECT:   We were, therefore, very much disgusted with their attitude.

INCORRECT: We were therefore very much disgusted with their attitude.
              [Missing commas around "therefore" as an interrupter]

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
MARGIE RULE [M-013] | Commas — "Obviously" | [CR-CRITICAL]
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
RULE: "Obviously" used as an interrupter must be surrounded by commas.

CORRECT:   It was, obviously, an error on his part.

INCORRECT: It was obviously an error on his part.
              [Missing commas around "obviously" as an interrupter]

NOTE: Whether to treat these adverbs as interrupters depends on the
      speaker's intent and intonation. When they function as parenthetical
      commentary, surround them with commas.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
MARGIE RULE [M-014] | Commas — "Again" | [CR-CRITICAL]
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
RULE: "Again" used as a transitional interrupter must be surrounded by
      commas when it is parenthetical.

CORRECT:   And, again, I was confronted with a very difficult choice.

INCORRECT: And again I was confronted with a very difficult choice.
              [Missing commas around "again"]

SECTION 6 — COMMAS: "TOO" AND "ALSO" AT END OF SENTENCE — NO COMMA
================================================================================

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
MARGIE RULE [M-015] | Commas — "Too" and "Also" | [CR-CRITICAL] [OVERRIDES GREGG]
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
RULE: Do NOT put a comma before "too," "also," or "as well" at the end of
      a sentence. No comma.

CORRECT:   She had to have the test too.
CORRECT:   He was a suspect also.
CORRECT:   Bacteria was found there as well.

INCORRECT: She had to have the test, too.
              [No comma before "too" at end of sentence]
INCORRECT: He was a suspect, also.
              [No comma before "also" at end of sentence]

NOTE: [OVERRIDES GREGG] — Traditional style guides (including Gregg) permit
      or even recommend a comma before "too" at end of sentence. Margie's
      rule: NO comma. This is the court reporting professional standard.

SECTION 7 — ABBREVIATIONS
================================================================================

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
MARGIE RULE [M-016] | Abbreviations — No Periods in Acronyms | [CR-CRITICAL] [OVERRIDES GREGG]
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
RULE: Do NOT use periods between letters in acronyms and initialisms.
      Write the letters solid, no periods.

CORRECT:   I work for IBM.
CORRECT:   She needs ten CEUs by June.

INCORRECT: I work for I.B.M.
              [No periods between letters]
INCORRECT: She needs ten CEU's.
              [No apostrophe for plural of an abbreviation]

NOTE: [OVERRIDES GREGG] — Older Gregg editions used I.B.M. style. Modern
      court reporting standard: no periods in acronyms.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
MARGIE RULE [M-017] | Abbreviations — No Apostrophe for Plural Abbreviations | [CR-CRITICAL]
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
RULE: Do NOT use an apostrophe to form the plural of an abbreviation.
      Simply add "s" with no apostrophe.

CORRECT:   She needs ten CEUs by June.

INCORRECT: She needs ten CEU's by June.
              [Apostrophe is wrong — CEU's implies possession, not plural]

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
MARGIE RULE [M-018] | Abbreviations — Suffixes Jr., Sr., III | [CR-CRITICAL] [OVERRIDES GREGG]
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
RULE: Do NOT put commas around "Jr." or "Sr." after a name. These are
      simply part of the name — no separating commas. Roman numerals
      (III, IV, etc.) following a name also take no commas.

CORRECT:   I spoke with Jack Macdonald Jr.
CORRECT:   Joseph Ray Sr. spoke to her.
CORRECT:   She married Hamilton Brown III.

INCORRECT: I spoke with Jack Macdonald, Jr.
              [No comma before Jr.]
INCORRECT: Joseph Ray, Sr., spoke to her.
              [No commas around Sr.]

NOTE: [OVERRIDES GREGG] — Older Gregg and traditional style used commas
      around Jr./Sr. Modern court reporting standard: no commas.

SECTION 8 — END PUNCTUATION: PERIODS
================================================================================

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
MARGIE RULE [M-019] | Periods — Complete Statement or Command | [CR-CRITICAL]
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
RULE: Put a period after a grammatically complete sentence that makes a
      statement or issues a command.

CORRECT:   Her plan does not include health benefits. I could not use it.
CORRECT:   I had very little to do with what happened later. That's it.
CORRECT:   He expected me to finish it. I didn't.
CORRECT:   I cannot be there. I am sorry.
CORRECT:   Leave now. You may still make it for the last train.
CORRECT:   Send it via email. I will check for it.
CORRECT:   Have it delivered. Thank you.

SECTION 9 — RUN-ON SENTENCES
================================================================================

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
MARGIE RULE [M-020] | Run-Ons — Definition and Fix | [CR-CRITICAL]
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
RULE: A run-on is the combination of two sentences that do NOT have a
      conjunction between them but that are punctuated with a comma or
      with nothing at all. Fix run-ons by using a period to separate the
      two complete sentences.

CORRECT:   Ray got into the work early in the day. Joe did not.
CORRECT:   I did not hear what you said. I'm sorry.
CORRECT:   We left with the understanding it would be handled. That's it.
CORRECT:   We do not need it at this point. Thank you.

INCORRECT: Ray got into the work early in the day, Joe did not.
              [Comma splice — run-on]
INCORRECT: I did not hear what you said, I'm sorry.
              [Comma splice — run-on]
INCORRECT: We left with the understanding it would be handled, that's it.
              [Comma splice — run-on]
INCORRECT: We do not need it at this point thank you.
              [No punctuation — run-on]

SECTION 10 — POLITE REQUESTS
================================================================================

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
MARGIE RULE [M-021] | Polite Requests — Period, Not Question Mark | [CR-CRITICAL]
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
RULE: A "polite request" asks for action or information but is NOT a true
      question — it cannot be answered with a simple "yes" or "no." A
      polite request correctly begins with "will," "would," "can," or
      "could." Use a PERIOD after a polite request, NOT a question mark.

CORRECT:   Q Would you turn to page 5 of the report that you have there.
CORRECT:   Q Will you tell me the names and ages of your children.
CORRECT:   Q Would you please take a seat in the witness stand.
CORRECT:   Q Would you state your name for the record, please.
CORRECT:   Q Can you hand the document you are holding to your counsel.
CORRECT:   Q Could you read the first paragraph there to yourself.
CORRECT:   Q Can you put your initials in the top corner of the page.

NOTE: The test — can it be answered "yes" or "no"? A true polite request
      CANNOT. "Would you state your name" — you cannot answer "no."
      Therefore it takes a period.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
MARGIE RULE [M-022] | Polite Requests — "Can you give me" — Period vs. Question Mark | [CR-CRITICAL]
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
RULE: When "can you give me" is a true polite request (asking them to
      provide the information), use a period. When it functions as a true
      yes-or-no question (testing ability), use a question mark.

CORRECT:   Q Can you give me the names.
              [Expecting an answer with names — polite request — period]
              A Jim, Robert, and Martin.

CORRECT:   Q Can you give me the names?
              [True question about ability — question mark]
              A I don't have a clue who those people were.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
MARGIE RULE [M-023] | Polite Requests — "May we have this marked" | [CR-CRITICAL]
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
RULE: When an attorney says "May we have this marked for identification,"
      this can be either a polite request (period) or a true question
      (question mark) depending on context and intonation.

CORRECT:   Q May we have this marked for identification.
              [Addressed to the court reporter — polite request — period]
CORRECT:   Q May we have this marked for identification?
              [Addressed to opposing counsel or the judge — true question —
              question mark]

NOTE: Use judgment based on who is being addressed and the intonation.

SECTION 11 — INDIRECT QUESTIONS VS. DIRECT QUESTIONS
================================================================================

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
MARGIE RULE [M-024] | Indirect Questions — Period, Not Question Mark | [CR-CRITICAL]
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
RULE: An indirect question always occurs in a dependent clause and does NOT
      use question word order. It has the "feel" of a question but not the
      "form" of a true question. Use a PERIOD after an indirect question,
      NOT a question mark.

DIRECT (question mark):   What time did you leave?
INDIRECT (period):        I am asking what time you left.

DIRECT (question mark):   When did the surgery take place?
INDIRECT (period):        I am wondering when the surgery took place.

DIRECT (question mark):   How far was it to his office?
INDIRECT (period):        I want to know how far it was to his office.

NOTE: The test — does the sentence use question word order (inverted subject/
      verb)? If "I am asking what time you left" — subject "you" comes
      BEFORE verb "left" — that is statement order. It is indirect.
      Use a period.

SECTION 12 — FRAGMENTS IN Q&A
================================================================================

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
MARGIE RULE [M-025] | Fragments — Definition | [CR-CRITICAL]
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
RULE: A fragment is an element that does not stand alone as a complete
      grammatical thought. Common fragment examples in court reporting:
      "because she is not here" / "on the corner" / "that he was there
      alone" / "near the base of the skull" / "very late yesterday
      afternoon."

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
MARGIE RULE [M-026] | Fragments — Punctuate Like the Complete Thought | [CR-CRITICAL]
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
RULE: Punctuate a fragment that stands for a complete thought the same way
      you would punctuate the complete thought it represents. A fragment
      standing for a statement gets a period. A fragment standing for a
      question gets a question mark.

CORRECT:   Not with him in that car. I was alone.
              ["Not with him in that car" = fragment standing for a statement]
CORRECT:   Eight. There were only eight.
CORRECT:   In the back. That's absolutely true.
CORRECT:   I intended to give that to you this morning. Sorry.

Q&A EXAMPLES:
CORRECT:   Q What time was it?
            A Around 10:00. We were late.
              ["Around 10:00" = fragment = period; new sentence follows]

CORRECT:   Q Who was driving?
            A Bob. Joe had been drinking.
              ["Bob" = fragment = period; new sentence follows]

CORRECT:   Q How far away were you standing?
            A About 50 feet. I was close.

CORRECT:   Q What day did this happen?
            A Friday. I think it was the 5th.

CORRECT:   Q Where did you see her?
            A At the beach. She was alone.

CORRECT:   Q Were you involved at all?
            A Minimally. I did little on that.

CORRECT:   Q When did you enter into that?
            A The 16th. Maybe it was the 17th.

NOTE: A SEMICOLON IS WRONG between a fragment answer and the follow-on
      sentence. Margie is explicit: "Would this be a good time for a
      semicolon? NO!" Use a period after the fragment, then a new sentence.

INCORRECT: A Around 10:00; we were late.
              [Semicolon is wrong here — period after the fragment]
INCORRECT: A Bob; Joe had been drinking.
              [Semicolon is wrong here]

SECTION 13 — ATTORNEY ECHOING WITNESS ANSWERS (Q repeats A)
================================================================================

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
MARGIE RULE [M-027] | Attorney Echo — Fragment + New Question | [CR-CRITICAL]
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
RULE: When an attorney echoes the witness's answer back and then asks a
      new question, the echoed fragment gets a period (it stands for a
      complete statement). The new question begins on the next line.

CORRECT:   Q How near to you was it?
            A It was no more than ten feet.
            Q No more than ten feet.
              Did you have a clear view?

CORRECT:   Q Who told you that?
            A It was Joe Ross.
            Q Joe Ross.
              Did you trust him?

CORRECT:   Q What did she indicate?
            A She indicated it would be no later than the 24th.
            Q The 24th.
              Was that a guaranteed date?

CORRECT:   Q When did you pay the final?
            A I think it was in May.
            Q In May.
              In your mind, was that it?

SECTION 14 — "WHAT ABOUT" AND "HOW ABOUT" QUESTIONS
================================================================================

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
MARGIE RULE [M-028] | "What About" / "How About" — Question Mark After Fragment | [CR-CRITICAL]
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
RULE: "What about X?" and "How about X?" are questions. The question mark
      goes after the fragment that forms the question, not after a follow-
      on sentence.

CORRECT:   Q What about John? Was he there?
CORRECT:   Q How about your sister? Did she help?

SECTION 15 — "OKAY," "ALL RIGHT," "FINE," "GREAT"
================================================================================

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
MARGIE RULE [M-029] | "Okay" — End of Sentence Punctuation | [CR-CRITICAL]
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
RULE: Put a period in front of "okay" when it comes at the end of a
      sentence (or as a standalone acknowledgment). Use an interrogative
      or period after "okay" depending on intonation.

CORRECT:   You say you insisted the doctor run additional tests. Okay.
              ["Okay" = acknowledgment — period before and after]
CORRECT:   I am going to show you several exhibits that were all marked at
              the same time yesterday. Okay?
              ["Okay?" = asking for confirmation — question mark after]

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
MARGIE RULE [M-030] | "Okay" — Mid-Sentence (Legitimate Question vs. Throwaway) | [CR-CRITICAL]
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
RULE: Surround "okay" with dashes in the middle of the sentence if it is
      asking a legitimate question. Otherwise treat it as a throwaway
      (use commas).

CORRECT:   You can speak with your attorney -- is that okay? -- before we
              go on.
              ["Is that okay?" = legitimate question — dashes]
CORRECT:   She was standing, okay, next to me and was, okay, acting weird,
              okay, and proceeded to start screaming.
              ["okay" = filler/throwaway — commas]

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
MARGIE RULE [M-031] | "Okay," "All Right," "Fine," "Great" — Transition Words | [CR-CRITICAL]
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
RULE: When an attorney uses "Okay," "All right," "Fine," or "Great" as a
      transition before asking the next question, punctuate them as their
      own complete units followed by a period. Paragraph after them if a
      new question follows.

CORRECT:   Q Was surgery inevitable?
            A It was a definite.
            Q Okay. When did you tell him?

CORRECT:   Q Were you alone in this?
            A I felt alone.
            Q All right. Fine. Did you reach...?

CORRECT:   Q Who led in sales?
            A Mark Johnson. He was always first.
            Q Mark Johnson. Okay. Great.
              Did he work from your branch?

NOTE: These transition words get periods, not commas, before the next
      question follows. They stand alone as complete units.

SECTION 16 — "YES" AND "NO" PUNCTUATION
================================================================================

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
MARGIE RULE [M-032] | "Yes"/"No" — Comma When What Follows Echoes the Question | [CR-CRITICAL]
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
RULE: When what follows "yes" or "no" ECHOES the words of the question,
      use a comma after "yes" or "no."

CORRECT:   Q Did she call you that night?
            A Yes, she did.
            A Yes, she called.

CORRECT:   Q Was he agitated?
            A No, he was not agitated.
            A No, he wasn't.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
MARGIE RULE [M-033] | "Yes"/"No" — Period When What Follows Does NOT Echo | [CR-CRITICAL]
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
RULE: When what follows "yes" or "no" does NOT echo the words of the
      question (introduces new information), use a PERIOD after "yes"
      or "no."

CORRECT:   Q Did she call you that night?
            A Yes. She asked about the loan.
            A Yes. She asked about my mom.

CORRECT:   Q Was he agitated?
            A No. He was amazingly calm.
            A No. He was in control.

CORRECT:   Q Was she in attendance?
            A No. But she sent a colleague.
            A Yes. Because she was in charge.

NOTE: A semicolon is NOT correct here. Margie is explicit: "Is this a time
      when a semicolon might work? NO."

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
MARGIE RULE [M-034] | "Yes"/"No" — Both Comma AND Period in Same Answer | [CR-CRITICAL]
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
RULE: An answer can have both comma (for the echo part) and period (for
      new information) in the same answer.

CORRECT:   Q Did she react well to the news?
            A Yes, she did. She embraced her new position.
            A No, she didn't. She broke down right away.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
MARGIE RULE [M-035] | "Yes"/"No" — At End of Answer | [CR-CRITICAL]
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
RULE: When "yes" or "no" comes at the END of an answer (witness adds it
      after the main answer), use a comma before it.

CORRECT:   Q Did you see her after she quit?
            A We met several times, yes.
            A I did not see her at all, no.

CORRECT:   Q Was he visibly upset?
            A He reacted rather violently, yes, and seemed to be over the edge.
            A I couldn't tell, no, that he was upset in any way.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
MARGIE RULE [M-036] | "Yes"/"No" — With Semicolons (Complex Sentence) | [CR-CRITICAL]
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
RULE: When a "yes" or "no" answer is part of a complex sentence with a
      semicolon already present, the "yes"/"no" still gets a comma (if
      echoing) or acts as its own unit.

CORRECT:   Q Did you send the form in?
            A I did not have it initially; but, yes, I did send it eventually.
            A She did not give it to me; and, no, I had no way to send it
              in otherwise.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
MARGIE RULE [M-037] | "Uh-huh," "Huh-uh," "Yep," "Nope" — Same Rules as Yes/No | [CR-CRITICAL]
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
RULE: The rules for "uh-huh," "huh-uh," "yep," "nope," and any other
      affirmative/negative responses are exactly the same as for "yes"
      and "no."

CORRECT:   Yep, I did accompany her.
              [Echoes — comma after "yep"]
CORRECT:   She was in surgery by the time I got there, uh-huh.
              [At end — comma before "uh-huh"]
CORRECT:   I made every effort; but, unh-unh, we weren't able to make
              that contact.

SECTION 17 — QUESTION MARKS: WHERE THE QUESTION IS FIRST ASKED
================================================================================

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
MARGIE RULE [M-038] | Question Mark — Place It Where Question Is First Asked | [CR-CRITICAL]
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
RULE: Put the question mark where the question is FIRST asked. If a
      clarifying phrase follows the question, it does not move the
      question mark.

CORRECT:   Q How many? Do you know?
CORRECT:   Q Who helped you? Your brother?
CORRECT:   Q How long was it? More than a month?
CORRECT:   Q What did you tell her? The truth or the lie you had been
              spreading?

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
MARGIE RULE [M-039] | Question Mark — Clarifying Phrase After the Question | [CR-CRITICAL]
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
RULE: When a clarifying phrase follows a question and that phrase is NOT
      itself a question, the question mark goes after the first question,
      and the clarifying phrase ends with a period (or can end with a
      question mark if it also asks something).

CORRECT:   Q Whom did you see? I am talking about when you first got there.
              [Clarifying phrase ends with period — it is a statement]
CORRECT:   Q How long was the surgery? I am referring to the surgery on
              the 4th.
CORRECT:   Q How much more did you spend? On your sister, I mean.
              [Fragment standing for a statement — period]

CORRECT:   Q Whom did you see? Do you recall?
              [Second part is a question — question mark]
CORRECT:   Q How long was the surgery? Do you have an estimate?
CORRECT:   Q How much more did you spend? Did you buy everything for her?

CORRECT:   Q Whom did you see? Your brother?
              [Fragment standing for a question — question mark]
CORRECT:   Q How long was the surgery? Several hours?
CORRECT:   Q How much more did you spend? More than $100?

SECTION 18 — SERIES OF QUESTIONS (STACKED QUESTIONS)
================================================================================

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
MARGIE RULE [M-040] | Stacked Questions — Multiple Question Marks | [CR-CRITICAL]
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
RULE: When an attorney asks a series of short questions, each question
      gets its own question mark. They can be stacked on separate lines
      or run together on one line.

CORRECT (stacked):
            Q Did you eat strawberries?
              blueberries?
              kiwi?
              papaya?

CORRECT (inline):
            Q Did you eat strawberries? blueberries? kiwi? papaya?
            Q Did you eat strawberries? Blueberries? Kiwi? Papaya?
              [Lowercase or capitalized continuation — both acceptable]

CORRECT:   Q Was the car you saw red? blue? gray? brown?
CORRECT:   Q What color was the car you saw? Red? Blue? Gray? Brown?
CORRECT:   Q Did you call 9-1-1? your doctor? the hospital?
CORRECT:   Q Which one did you consider purchasing? The large one?
              The small one? One of the others?
CORRECT:   Q Who did that work for you? Your admin assistant? Your clerk?
              Your bookkeeper?
CORRECT:   Q How did you find out? Who told you? The doctor?

SECTION 19 — TAG CLAUSES
================================================================================

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
MARGIE RULE [M-041] | Tag Clauses — Definition | [CR-CRITICAL]
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
RULE: A "tag clause" is added to a sentence that is a statement in order
      to turn it into a question. There are two types:
      (1) those that echo the original statement (wasn't he / was it /
          had they not)
      (2) those that use "right," "true," or "correct"

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
MARGIE RULE [M-042] | Tag Clauses — Echo Type (Comma) | [CR-CRITICAL]
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
RULE: When the tag clause echoes the statement (e.g., "wasn't he," "was
      it," "had they not"), use COMMAS to set it off.

CORRECT:   He was with the company ten years, wasn't he?
CORRECT:   Her leg was not broken, was it?
CORRECT:   They had signed the papers for the loan, had they not?
CORRECT:   I am correct, am I not?

CORRECT (with continuation):
           He was with the company ten years, wasn't he, when you
              discovered the second set of books?
           Her leg was not broken, was it, at the time you examined her?

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
MARGIE RULE [M-043] | Tag Clauses — Independent Subject/Verb at End (Semicolon) | [CR-CRITICAL]
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
RULE: When the tag clause has its own subject and verb and comes at the
      END of the sentence, use a SEMICOLON in front of it.

CORRECT:   He was with the company ten years; is that correct?
CORRECT:   Her leg was not broken; is that right?
CORRECT:   They had signed the papers for the loan; isn't that true?

LONGER EXAMPLE:
CORRECT:   You testified last Friday in your deposition that the man you
              saw was wearing a brown winter coat that seemed to be
              oversized and that he had his hands in the pockets and that
              there was a bulge in the right-side pocket. Is that correct?
              [Full stop + new sentence when the main sentence is very long]

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
MARGIE RULE [M-044] | Tag Clauses — Independent Subject/Verb in MIDDLE (Dashes) | [CR-CRITICAL]
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
RULE: When the tag clause has its own subject and verb and comes in the
      MIDDLE of the sentence, use a pair of DASHES around it.

CORRECT:   He was with the company ten years -- is that correct? -- when
              you discovered the second set of books?
CORRECT:   Her leg was not broken -- is that right? -- when you examined
              her?

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
MARGIE RULE [M-045] | Tag Clauses — Fragment Form with Semicolon | [CR-CRITICAL]
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
RULE: Single-word tag fragments ("correct?" / "right?" / "true?") follow
      the same rule as the full clause form. Use a semicolon before them.

CORRECT:   He was with the company ten years; correct?
CORRECT:   Her leg was not broken; right?
CORRECT:   They had signed the papers for the loan; true?

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
MARGIE RULE [M-046] | Tag Clauses — "Is that fair?" / "Is that what you are saying?" | [CR-CRITICAL]
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
RULE: When a follow-on question like "Is that fair?" or "Is that what you
      are saying?" comes after a completed statement, put a PERIOD in
      front of it and let it stand as its own question.

CORRECT:   She regularly exceeded 40 hours a week. Is that fair?
CORRECT:   You lived just a block from the beach. Is that what you are
              saying?
CORRECT:   There were nine children. Is that what I am to understand?

SECTION 20 — OBJECTIONS
================================================================================

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
MARGIE RULE [M-047] | Objections — Correct Punctuation | [CR-CRITICAL]
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
RULE: "Objection" ends with a period. The grounds follow as a list,
      separated by semicolons. Each ground is capitalized after the
      first semicolon if it stands alone.

CORRECT:   Objection. Asked and answered; compound, unintelligible.
              [Colon or dash separations are also used — see variants below]
CORRECT:   Objection: Asked and answered; compound; unintelligible.
CORRECT:   Objection. Asked and answered; Compound. Unintelligible.

NOTE: Multiple formats exist in the field. The key rule is that "Objection"
      stands as its own sentence (period), and the grounds follow with
      internal semicolons to separate them.

SECTION 21 — PREPOSITIONAL PHRASES AT BEGINNING OF SENTENCE
================================================================================

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
MARGIE RULE [M-048] | Prepositional Phrases — Short Modifier at Beginning, No Comma | [CR-CRITICAL]
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
RULE: When there is one short prepositional phrase that is a simple
      modifier at the beginning of a sentence, do NOT use a comma after it.
      The test is NOT the number of words — it is the FUNCTION. If it
      simply modifies, no comma.

CORRECT:   After our lunch we intended to inquire about it.
CORRECT:   At noon she called to tell us.
CORRECT:   During May he left on vacation.
CORRECT:   On Friday it was set to go into effect.
CORRECT:   In June we were at the crucial point.

NOTE: Do NOT count words to decide. The question is: does the phrase
      simply modify, or does it do something more? If it simply modifies,
      no comma.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
MARGIE RULE [M-049] | Prepositional Phrases — When They Do NOT Just Modify (Comma Required) | [CR-CRITICAL]
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
RULE: When a prepositional phrase at the beginning of a sentence does
      more than simply modify (e.g., it transitions, qualifies, or
      stands in for a larger idea), use a comma after it.

CORRECT:   In 2012, 450,000 cartons were delivered to the company.
              [Comma prevents misreading: "In 2012 450,000..." — confusing]
CORRECT:   In general, elections were held on Tuesdays.
CORRECT:   For Arthur, Joseph would do anything.
CORRECT:   According to her, there was no one qualified for the job.
CORRECT:   In my opinion, he should be court martialed.
CORRECT:   To his recollection, the money was not enough.
CORRECT:   At any rate, it did not make a difference.
CORRECT:   By the way, how much did they charge?
CORRECT:   On the other hand, it was not a great fit for me.

SECTION 22 — PARENTHETICALS (GENERAL)
================================================================================

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
MARGIE RULE [M-050] | Parentheticals — Definition and Core Rule | [CR-CRITICAL]
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
RULE: A "parenthetical" is an element (word, phrase, or clause) that does
      not impact the meaning of the sentence. A parenthetical can be
      moved anywhere within the sentence. ALWAYS surround a parenthetical
      with punctuation.

CORRECT:   She was, by the way, also a jerk.
CORRECT:   And, gee, I haven't seen her in years.
CORRECT:   He just didn't listen, in my judgment.
CORRECT:   Clearly, this is a case of not caring.
CORRECT:   It didn't, however, seem to matter.

CORRECT (same parenthetical, multiple positions):
           Of course, I intended to speak up.
           I, of course, intended to speak up.
           I intended, of course, to speak up.
           I intended to speak up, of course.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
MARGIE RULE [M-051] | Parentheticals — "Like," "You Know," "Gee," "Say" | [CR-CRITICAL]
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
RULE: Filler words ("like," "you know," "gee," "say") are parentheticals
      and must be set off with commas wherever they appear.

CORRECT:   He was, like, about 15.
CORRECT:   They walked, like, you know, together for, like, three blocks.
CORRECT:   He, like, really, you know, was alone.
CORRECT:   Therefore, she, you know, was, like, gee, getting, you know,
              near, say, 50.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
MARGIE RULE [M-052] | Parentheticals — "First," "Second," "Secondly" as Interrupters | [CR-CRITICAL]
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
RULE: When ordinals like "first," "second," "secondly," "one," "two,"
      "three" are used as parenthetical sequence markers, surround them
      with commas.

CORRECT:   There was really nothing else to do, secondly.
CORRECT:   It was, first, the thing she felt she had to do.
CORRECT:   She resigned because, one, she was pregnant and because, two,
              she was sick and because, three, she was going to the doctor.

CORRECT (as part of a list introduced by a colon):
           These were the reasons for the sequence of events: First, we
              had to go there; second, we had...
           These were the reasons: first, the need to go there; second,
              the need to...

SECTION 23 — "THAT IS," "I.E.," "FOR EXAMPLE," "E.G.," "NAMELY," ETC.
================================================================================

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
MARGIE RULE [M-053] | Explanatory Parentheticals — Punctuation Depends on What Follows | [CR-CRITICAL]
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
RULE: Words and phrases like "that is," "i.e.," "for example," "e.g.,"
      "namely," "to wit," "for instance," "in other words" introduce
      explanatory material. The punctuation before and after them depends
      on what comes after.

VARIANT A — At the end, with a FRAGMENT after it — use a pair of commas:
CORRECT:   ...that month, that is, August...
CORRECT:   ...her sister, in other words, Jan...
CORRECT:   ...my company, i.e., Microsoft...
CORRECT:   ...for that car, for example, for that Toyota...

VARIANT B — At the end, with a SENTENCE after it — semicolon before, comma after:
CORRECT:   ...that month; that is, it was August that we were there.
CORRECT:   ...her sister; in other words, we saw Jan that night.
CORRECT:   ...my company; i.e., Microsoft has joined in the effort.

VARIANT C — At the end, with a LIST after it — colon before, comma after:
CORRECT:   ...several months: for example, June, September, and November.
CORRECT:   ...her siblings: in other words, Joan, Jeanine, Jack, and Jerry.
CORRECT:   ...all three bases: that is, first, second, and third.

VARIANT D — After a question — question mark before, comma after:
CORRECT:   Did you talk to him when he arrived? That is, when he came in
              from the trip.
CORRECT:   Who helped with the work? In other words, did the financials.
CORRECT:   Who was standing there? For instance, your boss.

VARIANT E — At the end, parenthetical RENAMES the subject — dash before, comma after:
CORRECT:   The CEO of the company had responsibilities beyond what the
              rest of us had -- that is, Ronald Harris.
CORRECT:   The surgeon rendered an opinion that was different from
              Dr. Ross's -- that is, Dr. Mencher.

VARIANT F — In the MIDDLE of the sentence — pair of dashes:
CORRECT:   The accountant we had -- that is, Reynaldo Martin -- was
              charged with a felony in the case.
CORRECT:   We owned two cars -- that is, a VW and a Ford -- before this
              incident.
CORRECT:   Similar surgeries -- e.g., tonsils, appendix -- are common.

NOTE: The dashes in Variant F follow the general rule: any time an element
      in the middle needs commas around it but also has punctuation inside
      it, replace the commas with dashes.

SECTION 24 — PLURALS
================================================================================

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
MARGIE RULE [M-054] | Plurals — Standard Rule (Adding -s or -es) | [CR-CRITICAL]
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
RULE: For words ending in -s, -x, -z, -ch, or -sh, add -es to form the
      plural. For all other words, add only -s.

CORRECT:   fox → foxes | mass → masses | church → churches | wish → wishes
           fez → fezes | girl → girls | storm → storms | iPhone → iPhones

Do NOT use an apostrophe for the plural of a regular word:
CORRECT:   witness → witnesses
INCORRECT: witness's or witness'
              [Apostrophe is never used for plural of a regular word]

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
MARGIE RULE [M-055] | Plurals — Proper Names (Do Not Change the Spelling) | [CR-CRITICAL]
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
RULE: Follow the same standard plural rules for proper names, but do NOT
      change the spelling of a proper name.

CORRECT:   Mario → Marios | Hindry → Hindrys | Burns → Burnses
           Rogers → Rogerses | Hodges → Hodgeses | Wells → Wellses

INCORRECT: The Wellses' → "the Wells'" [wrong — should be "the Wellses'"]
INCORRECT: Using apostrophes to form plurals of names:
           The Oliver's → wrong; should be "the Olivers"

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
MARGIE RULE [M-056] | Plurals — "The" + Surname Always Takes Plural | [CR-CRITICAL]
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
RULE: When "the" precedes a surname, always make the name plural.

CORRECT:   I was with the Wellses.
CORRECT:   I was with the Rosses.
CORRECT:   I was with the Sanchezes.
CORRECT:   I was with the Hodgkines.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
MARGIE RULE [M-057] | Plurals — Compound Nouns (Pluralize the Main Noun) | [CR-CRITICAL]
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
RULE: If a compound noun has more than one word, make the MAIN noun plural.

CORRECT:   sister-in-law → sisters-in-law
           attorney general → attorneys general
           court martial → courts martial

SECTION 25 — POSSESSIVES
================================================================================

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
MARGIE RULE [M-058] | Possessives — Singular Possessive: Always Add 's | [CR-CRITICAL] [OVERRIDES GREGG]
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
RULE: For EVERY word you want to make singular possessive, add apostrophe
      + s to the singular form — NO EXCEPTIONS. This includes words that
      already end in s.

CORRECT:   boy → boy's | woman → woman's | class → class's
           witness → witness's | boss → boss's | fox → fox's
           Mr. Ross → Mr. Ross's | Ms. Sanchez → Ms. Sanchez's
           Mrs. Wells → Mrs. Wells's | Mr. Hodges → Mr. Hodges's

NOTE: [OVERRIDES GREGG] — Some style guides say to add only an apostrophe
      after singular words ending in s. Margie's rule: always add 's —
      no exceptions.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
MARGIE RULE [M-059] | Possessives — Plural Possessive: Add Only Apostrophe (Words Ending in S) | [CR-CRITICAL]
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
RULE: For the plural possessive of words that END in s, add ONLY the
      apostrophe (most plurals do end in s).

CORRECT:   girls → girls' | friends → friends' | witnesses → witnesses'
           classes → classes' | the Rosses → the Rosses'
           the Wellses → the Wellses' | the Hodgeses → the Hodgeses'

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
MARGIE RULE [M-060] | Possessives — Plural Possessive: Add 's (Words NOT Ending in S) | [CR-CRITICAL]
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
RULE: For the plural possessive of words that do NOT end in s, add 's.

CORRECT:   children → children's | men → men's | oxen → oxen's
           women → women's

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
MARGIE RULE [M-061] | Possessives — Compound Nouns: Apostrophe on the End | [CR-CRITICAL]
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
RULE: Add all possessive apostrophes onto the END of the word.

CORRECT:   singular: brother-in-law → brother-in-law's
           plural:   brothers-in-law → brothers-in-law's
           singular: aide-de-camp → aide-de-camp's
           plural:   aides-de-camp → aides-de-camp's

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
MARGIE RULE [M-062] | Possessives — The Four Forms (Homophones) | [CR-CRITICAL]
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
RULE: Be sure you have the correct form of the base word (singular or
      plural) before making it possessive. These are homophones —
      pick the correct form for context.

EXAMPLES:
   boy / boy's / boys / boys'
   class / class's / classes / classes'
   Mr. Hess / Mr. Hess's / the Hesses / the Hesses'
   Ms. Hodges / Ms. Hodges's / the Hodgeses / the Hodgeses'

CORRECT:   my manager's assistants [one manager]
           all the managers' assistants [multiple managers]
           one witness's statements [one witness]
           two witnesses' statements [multiple witnesses]
           my son's birthday [one son]
           my sons' birthdays [multiple sons]

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
MARGIE RULE [M-063] | Possessives — Do Not Use Possessive When You Just Want Plural | [CR-CRITICAL]
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
RULE: Do not use the possessive form when you just want the plural.

CORRECT:   A card from the Olivers. [the family — plural]
INCORRECT: A card from the Oliver's. [wrong — apostrophe implies possession]

CORRECT:   We went with the Rays last night. [the people, the family]
CORRECT:   We were at the Rays' last night. [their house — possessive]

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
MARGIE RULE [M-064] | Possessives — Q&A: "Mr. Hughes' car" vs. "Mr. Hughes's car" | [CR-CRITICAL]
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
RULE: In Q&A, when Q uses a possessive and A echoes it, both should use
      the correct singular possessive form (apostrophe + s).

CORRECT:   Q Did you see Mr. Hughes's car?
            A I did not see Mr. Hughes's car.

NOTE: "Mr. Hughes'" (apostrophe only) would be incorrect under Margie's
      rule — add 's to ALL singular possessives, no exceptions.

SECTION 26 — POSSESSIVE ADJECTIVES OF TIME, DISTANCE, AMOUNT, VALUE
================================================================================

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
MARGIE RULE [M-065] | Possessives — Time/Distance/Amount/Value Adjectives | [CR-CRITICAL]
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
RULE: When an adjective that is right in front of a noun has an "s" on it
      AND it denotes time, distance, amount, or value, use an apostrophe.
      Use apostrophe + s for singular amounts; apostrophe only for plural
      amounts.

SINGULAR (apostrophe + s):
CORRECT:   one year's time | a week's vacation | an hour's wait
           one week's pay | a stone's throw

PLURAL (apostrophe only):
CORRECT:   three weeks' duration | 20 minutes' delay | a few dollars' worth
           three days' duration

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
MARGIE RULE [M-066] | Possessives — Hyphenation When There Is No 's' | [CR-CRITICAL]
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
RULE: If the adjective form does NOT use an "s" (and therefore has no
      apostrophe), the combination is HYPHENATED.

CORRECT:   one week's vacation  OR  one-week vacation
           15 minutes' delay    OR  15-minute delay

NOTE: The two forms are equivalent alternatives. Choose one consistently.

SECTION 27 — MISCELLANEOUS COURT REPORTING NOTES
================================================================================

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
MARGIE RULE [M-067] | Spoken Spelling / Addresses for the Record | [CR-CRITICAL]
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
RULE: When a witness spells something or gives an email address for the
      record, transcribe it in a readable format. Use standard conventions.

CORRECT (spoken letter + word):   J, as in "John"; my last name...
CORRECT (time):                   3:00 [NOT "3:00 o'clock" — redundant]
CORRECT (fractions):              and a half [NOT "and one half" — unnecessary]
CORRECT (email):                  jmiller766@yahoo.com
                                  [NOT "j m i l l e r 766 at yahoo dot com"]

NOTE: These are discussion points from Margie's seminar on how to render
      spoken technical information cleanly in the transcript.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
MARGIE RULE [M-068] | Money — Two Acceptable Plurals | [CR-CRITICAL]
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
RULE: The plural of "money" has two acceptable spellings:
      "monies" (used for financial issues) and "moneys" (used for
      different kinds of money). Both are correct.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
MARGIE RULE [M-069] | "Fish" vs. "Fishes" |
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
RULE: "Fish" is the plural when counting the number. "Fishes" is the plural
      when referring to different types/species.

CORRECT:   She caught three fish. [counting number]
CORRECT:   The aquarium houses many fishes. [types of fish]

SECTION 28 — DASHES IN COURT REPORTING
================================================================================

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
MARGIE RULE [M-070] | Dashes — When Commas Would Cause Confusion | [CR-CRITICAL]
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
RULE: Any time an element in the MIDDLE of a sentence needs commas around
      it but also has punctuation INSIDE it (creating confusion), replace
      the commas with dashes.

CORRECT:   The accountant we had -- that is, Reynaldo Martin -- was charged
              with a felony.
              [Dashes replace the commas that would otherwise surround the
              "that is, Reynaldo Martin" element, because the comma after
              "that is" is already inside the element]

CORRECT:   We owned two cars -- that is, a VW and a Ford -- before this
              incident.
CORRECT:   Similar surgeries -- e.g., tonsils, appendix -- are common.

QUICK REFERENCE — MARGIE'S "NEVER DO THIS" LIST
================================================================================

NEVER put a comma between month and year (June 2012, NOT June, 2012)
NEVER put a comma before "too" or "also" at end of sentence
NEVER put a comma before or after Jr./Sr./III in a name
NEVER use periods in acronyms (IBM, not I.B.M.)
NEVER use an apostrophe to form the plural of abbreviations (CEUs, not CEU's)
NEVER use an apostrophe to form the plural of a regular word
NEVER use a semicolon between a fragment answer and its follow-on sentence
NEVER use a comma splice (two sentences joined only by a comma)
NEVER omit the closing comma after a state name or year in mid-sentence
NEVER put a question mark after an indirect question
NEVER put a question mark after a polite request

ALWAYS close city/state with a second comma when the sentence continues
ALWAYS close full date (m/d/y) with a second comma when sentence continues
ALWAYS surround parenthetical elements with punctuation (never half-pair)
ALWAYS add 's for singular possessive — no exceptions, even for words
   ending in s
ALWAYS use a period after a polite request (will you / would you / can you)
ALWAYS place the question mark where the question is FIRST asked
ALWAYS use a period (not semicolon) between a Q&A fragment and follow-on
ALWAYS surround "Jr." and "Sr." with nothing (no commas, no special treatment)
