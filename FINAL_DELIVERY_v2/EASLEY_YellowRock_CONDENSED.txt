SEGMENT 5 - FINANCIAL DIFFICULTIES / RESTRUCTURING [~Pages 75-115]

Q: Sunny Solar loan - when due?
A: Maturity approximately January 29, 2021. White Top could not repay.
   COVID-19 pandemic and drop in oil prices attributed.

Q: Production trajectory?
A: March 2020: ~409 barrels/day. January 2021: ~140 barrels equivalent/day
   (drop of roughly two-thirds). December 2021: 70 barrels/day.

Q: Cash flow January 2021?
A: Generating approximately $20,000/month positive cash flow before debt
   service and principal repayment. The business could survive before
   consideration of debt.

Q: What would it take to reach 1,000 BOE/day?
A: $6 million in capital for additional wells and recompletions.

Q: What was the main obstacle?
A: Money. Already had the prospects identified and ranked.

Q: Hurricane Laura impact?
A: August 2020 direct strike on Sulphur and the Sulphur Mines Field.
   Electricity completely knocked out. Two power stations destroyed and had to
   be replaced. Then Hurricane Delta and tropical storms over 2-3 months.
   Field shut in -- no production, no revenue. Had to source electrical
   contractor from New Mexico. Still $70,000 in outstanding storm damage
   repairs as of December 31, 2021.

Q: Chapter 11 plan?
A: Not a formal plan. Robin French's June 2021 email described it as exploring
   a reorganization plan. Dick Fuqua retained as bankruptcy counsel to assess.
   But it was a potential alternative, not a formal plan.

Q: Sunny Solar restructuring -- what happened?
A: August 2021 agreement. Sunny Solar takes majority/controlling interest.
   French and Easley replaced as managers by Jonathan Barrett. Easley and
   French remained on board.

Q: Payroll taxes?
A: As of December 2021, hundreds of thousands of dollars in payroll taxes had
   been withheld from employees but not paid to government.

Q: New ownership team composition?
A: Jonathan Barrett (chief executive through Sunny Solar); Matt Steele
   (advisory); Jonathan Love (land and operations/COO); Jennifer Saltsman
   (reservoir engineer); Mickey McGhee (geophysicist). Pumpers Sebastian
   Garcia and Veronica Naito (N-a-i-t-o) remained.

Q: What did new ownership accomplish?
A: New team much more intense/rigorous technically. Identified some new
   drilling prospects -- fresh look at recompletion opportunities using analogy

   team spent). Drilled Fee 1031 (completed as commercial wet well, production
   not great).

Q: When did permits stop?
A: DNR stopped issuing drilling and recompletion permits in late 2022/early
   2023. Development of the field, in Easley's words, "brought to a screeching
   halt."

SEGMENT 7 - PRESSURE LOSS EVENT / THEORIES [~Pages 30-65]

Q: Understanding of the pressure loss event?
A: Layman's understanding: massive amounts of salt water had to be pumped into
   the particular cavern to offset the pressure losses. Saying it another way:
   there was a leak.

Q: When did you first learn of it?
A: First quarter of 2023. Briefed informally by Jonathan Love, then formal
   board meeting briefings.

Q: Theories you've heard as to the cause?
A: (1) Salt water from Yellow Rock's two SWD wells (S4/S7) was injected in
   excess near the salt, possibly dissolving it from outside -- Easley says
   this was put to rest after December 2019 DNR meeting. (2) A directional
   well drilled too close to/penetrating the salt dome. Easley says his
   personal view is the SWD wells had no impact at all.

Q: November/December 2019 Baton Rouge meeting with Commissioner Ieyoub?
A: Westlake and others attended. Concerned the S4 and S7 salt water disposal
   wells should be required to cease injection and plug and abandon. Yellow
   Rock proposed at that meeting to materially curtail injection volumes;
   regulatory action placed on hold. Also received agreement that Yellow Rock
   could convert an existing well bore away from the cavern area for additional
   disposal capacity.

Q: DNR's 1,320-foot rule?
A: Stephen Lee, manager of DNR Mines & Minerals Division, communicated
   informally that DNR would not consider siting an SWD closer than 1,320 feet
   from the edge of the dome. Sometimes even that is considered too close. Not
   in the written rules -- this was informal guidance.

Q: Is the pressure loss event temporary or permanent?
A: Based on my understanding at that point, and evidenced by the moratorium
   DNR put on drilling and recompletions, I would say it appears to be a
   potential permanent impact. No foreseeable opportunity to do any development
   in the field. A six-foot-tall levee was put all the way around the field.

SEGMENT 8 - PROXIMITY TO SALT / CAVERN DATA [~Pages 155-185]

Q: April 2019 email to Todd Bertelot -- you wanted 3D cavern visualization
   software?
A: Yes. Wanted to understand where Boardwalk/Westlake caverns were subsurface
   relative to proposed Yellow Rock drilling, about 7,000 feet down. Part of
   a practice of circulating proposed well coordinates to Boardwalk and Westlake
   to coordinate and obtain their concurrence (obtained in all instances).

Q: Was the location of caverns relevant to where Yellow Rock could safely
   drill?
A: It was information we certainly wanted to know about. There were no
   prohibitions per se on drilling up to the edge of the cavern, but we wanted
   to cover what we didn't know below ground so we wouldn't be surprised.

Q: Were any DNR permits denied for Yellow Rock's 71 identified prospects?
A: Not denied -- placed in limbo. None issued since Fee 1031.
