CATEGORY 1: PERIODS AND TERMINAL PUNCTUATION
================================================================================

RULE 1.1 — Period after a complete declarative or imperative sentence
  A complete statement ends with a period.
  §101
  EXAMPLE:
    The witness identified the document.
  NOTE: In deposition Q&A, each answer that is a complete sentence ends with
  a period. Fragments that are complete thoughts also end with periods (§101b).

RULE 1.2 — Period after an elliptical (incomplete) sentence
  A sentence that is grammatically incomplete but expresses a complete thought
  takes a period, not a comma or nothing.
  §101b
  EXAMPLES:
    Q. Did you sign the contract?
    A. Yes.    [NOT: Yes,]
    A. Possibly.
    A. No. Not at all.

RULE 1.3 — Period with abbreviations in deposition context
  Standard abbreviations (Mr., Dr., Inc., etc.) take periods per Gregg §103.
  In deposition testimony, spell out titles on first use per court convention,
  but follow standard period rules when abbreviations appear.
  §103

RULE 1.4 — Decimal points
  Use a period as a decimal point in numbers.
  §107
  EXAMPLE:
    The fracture gradient was 12.5 ppg.
    The interest rate was 6.25 percent.

RULE 1.5 — No period after Q and A speaker labels [CR-CRITICAL]
  In the Q&A format of depositions, the speaker label (Q., A.) is followed by
  a space and then the spoken text. The label itself uses a period, but no
  additional terminal period is needed for the label — it IS a period. The
  answer text that follows then ends with its own terminal punctuation.
  §199e, §270
  EXAMPLE:
    Q. Did you attend the meeting on March 5th?
    A. Yes, I did.

CATEGORY 2: COMMAS
================================================================================

RULE 2.1 — Comma before coordinating conjunction in compound sentence [CR-CRITICAL]
  Use a comma before and, but, or, nor, for, yet, so when they join two
  independent clauses.
  §133
  EXAMPLE:
    We reviewed the documents, and the attorney confirmed the dates.
    The deponent answered each question, but she could not recall the date.
  EXCEPTION: If both independent clauses are very short (four words or fewer
  each), the comma may be omitted.

RULE 2.2 — Comma after introductory dependent clause
  A dependent clause that introduces a main clause is followed by a comma.
  §135
  EXAMPLE:
    After the deposition concluded, the attorneys conferred.
    Because the witness was unavailable, the deposition was rescheduled.

RULE 2.3 — Comma after introductory phrase (five or more words)
  A long introductory phrase (five or more words) is followed by a comma.
  §138
  EXAMPLE:
    At the conclusion of the drilling operation, the well was cemented.
    In the years following the accident, the company changed its procedures.
  NOTE: Short introductory prepositional phrases (fewer than five words) do
  not require a comma unless needed for clarity.

RULE 2.4 — Comma with nonessential (nonrestrictive) clauses and phrases [CR-CRITICAL]
  Set off with commas any clause or phrase that can be removed without
  changing the essential meaning of the sentence.
  §141
  EXAMPLES:
    The engineer, who had worked on the project for ten years, testified first.
    The report, dated March 15th, was entered as Exhibit 3.
  CONTRAST (essential — no commas):
    The engineer who designed the system testified that it was safe.
    [The "who" clause identifies WHICH engineer — it is essential.]

RULE 2.5 — Comma in a series of three or more [CR-CRITICAL]
  Use a comma after each item in a series of three or more, including before
  the final "and" or "or" (serial/Oxford comma).
  §132
  EXAMPLE:
    The witness reviewed the drawings, the specifications, and the safety manual.

RULE 2.6 — Comma between coordinate adjectives
  When two or more adjectives each independently modify the noun, separate
  them with commas.
  §151
  TEST: If "and" fits naturally between the adjectives, use a comma.
  EXAMPLE:
    It was a thorough, well-organized deposition.
    [thorough AND well-organized → comma]
  NO COMMA when the first adjective modifies the combined second-adjective+noun:
    She wore a bright red dress. [bright modifies "red dress" as a unit → no comma]

RULE 2.7 — Comma with parenthetical and transitional expressions
  Words and phrases that interrupt the flow of a sentence are set off by commas.
  §139
  EXAMPLES:
    The deponent, in my opinion, was evasive on that point.
    We will, of course, produce all requested documents.
    This practice, however, did not comply with industry standards.

RULE 2.8 — Comma with appositives
  An appositive (a noun or noun phrase that renames another noun) is set off
  by commas when it is nonessential.
  §142
  EXAMPLE:
    Mr. Johnson, the project manager, supervised the operation.
    Exhibit 12, the drilling log, was admitted into evidence.
  ESSENTIAL appositive (no commas):
    My colleague Scott reviewed the file. [identifies which colleague]

RULE 2.9 — Comma in direct address
  When the speaker directly addresses a person by name or title, set off with
  commas.
  §129
  EXAMPLE:
    Q. Mr. Smith, can you describe what happened?
    A. Yes, Counsel, I can.

RULE 2.10 — No comma between subject and verb
  Do not place a comma between a subject and its verb, no matter how long
  the subject.
  §163
  WRONG:  The manager who oversaw the entire drilling project, signed the report.
  RIGHT:  The manager who oversaw the entire drilling project signed the report.

CATEGORY 3: SEMICOLONS
================================================================================

RULE 3.1 — Semicolon between independent clauses without a conjunction
  When two independent clauses are closely related and no coordinating
  conjunction joins them, use a semicolon.
  §176
  EXAMPLE:
    The witness confirmed the date; the records supported her recollection.

RULE 3.2 — Semicolon with transitional expressions between clauses [CR-CRITICAL]
  When a transitional expression (however, therefore, moreover, consequently,
  in addition, etc.) joins two independent clauses, use a semicolon before the
  transitional expression and a comma after it.
  §178
  EXAMPLE:
    The company followed standard procedures; however, the outcome was not as expected.
    The deponent could not recall the date; therefore, the exhibit was used to refresh her memory.
  [CR-CRITICAL]: This semicolon+transitional pattern appears in complex Q&A
  answers and must be correctly punctuated, not replaced with a comma.

RULE 3.3 — Semicolon in a series when items contain internal commas [CR-CRITICAL]
  When items in a series already contain commas, use semicolons to separate
  the items.
  §181
  EXAMPLE:
    The project involved engineers from Houston, Texas; New Orleans, Louisiana; and
    Denver, Colorado.
    The parties include Johnson & Associates, Inc.; Meridian Energy, LLC; and the
    State of Louisiana.

RULE 3.4 — Semicolon before a coordinating conjunction joining long clauses
  A semicolon may be used before and, but, or, nor when the clauses being
  joined are long or contain internal commas.
  §179

CATEGORY 4: COLONS
================================================================================

RULE 4.1 — Colon before a list or enumeration
  Use a colon after a complete clause that introduces a list or series.
  §187
  EXAMPLE:
    The witness identified three problems: inadequate training, improper equipment,
    and lack of supervision.
  NOTE: Do NOT use a colon when the list flows directly from a verb or
  preposition (the list is the object of the verb).
  WRONG:  The three problems were: training, equipment, and supervision.
  RIGHT:  The three problems were training, equipment, and supervision.

RULE 4.2 — Colon before an explanatory or amplifying clause
  Use a colon when the second clause explains or amplifies the first.
  §188
  EXAMPLE:
    The reason was simple: no one had followed the established protocol.

RULE 4.3 — Colon in time expressions
  Use a colon between hours and minutes.
  §193
  EXAMPLE:
    The meeting began at 9:30 a.m.
    The deposition was recessed at 11:45 a.m.

RULE 4.4 — Capitalization after a colon
  Capitalize the first word after a colon when it begins a complete sentence
  that stands independently (especially a formal rule or statement). When
  the matter following the colon is not a complete sentence, do not capitalize
  unless it is a proper noun.
  §197
  EXAMPLES:
    The rule is clear: No employee may alter the logs.   [Complete sentence → cap]
    He listed three items: a map, a compass, and a notebook.   [Not a sentence → no cap]

RULE 4.5 — Colon in Q&A transcript format [CR-CRITICAL]
  In deposition transcripts, the speaker label is followed by a colon (or a
  period, per court convention), not a comma or dash. The first word of the
  spoken text is capitalized.
  §199e
  EXAMPLE:
    Q.  Did you review the document?
    A.  Yes, I reviewed it.
  [CR-CRITICAL]: Q and A are always capitalized in the Q&A label. See also
  Category 10, Rule 10.8.

CATEGORY 5: QUESTION MARKS AND EXCLAMATION POINTS
================================================================================

RULE 5.1 — Question mark after a direct question
  A direct question ends with a question mark.
  §110
  EXAMPLE:
    Q. Did you sign the document?
    Can you explain how the accident occurred?

RULE 5.2 — Period (not question mark) after indirect question
  An indirect question is a declarative statement and ends with a period.
  §112
  EXAMPLE:
    Counsel asked whether the witness had signed the document.   [NOT a question mark]
    He wanted to know who had authorized the change.

RULE 5.3 — Independent question within a sentence takes a question mark [CR-CRITICAL]
  When a question is embedded within a sentence and stands as an independent
  question, it may take a question mark and begin with a capital letter.
  §111, §301d
  EXAMPLE:
    The attorney asked the central question: Who authorized the change?
    The issue before the court was, Did the company know of the hazard?

RULE 5.4 — Question mark with tag questions
  A question that ends with a tag (isn't it? didn't you? right?) takes a
  question mark.
  §110
  EXAMPLE:
    A. You were there that day, weren't you?
    A. That's what the report says, isn't it?

RULE 5.5 — Exclamation points — rare in deposition transcripts
  Exclamation points are rarely appropriate in deposition transcripts.
  Testimony is transcribed neutrally. An exclamation point may appear only
  if a witness's utterance was clearly exclamatory in character.
  §121

CATEGORY 6: DASHES — EM DASH AND EN DASH
================================================================================

[CR-CRITICAL — entire category]

RULE 6.1 — Em dash for abrupt break or interruption in speech [SEEN IN TEST] [CR-CRITICAL]
  Use an em dash (—) when a speaker is interrupted mid-sentence. This is the
  MOST COMMON em dash use in deposition transcripts.
  §207
  FORMAT: No space before or after the em dash (§216).
  EXAMPLE:
    A. We were following the standard pro—
    Q. Let me stop you there.

    A. The valve was—
    Q. Objection. Move on.
  [SEEN IN TEST]: Raw steno often produces double hyphens (__) or (--) for
  the em dash. These MUST be converted to a true em dash (—) with no
  surrounding spaces.
  CORRECTION PATTERN: __ → —   or   -- → —

RULE 6.2 — Em dash for trailing off (unfinished thought) [SEEN IN TEST]
  Use an em dash when a speaker trails off without completing a sentence.
  Some reporters distinguish trailing-off (em dash) from deliberate pause
  (ellipsis); follow Layer 2/Layer 3 preference when specified.
  §207
  EXAMPLE:
    A. I'm not sure if I—
    [deponent pauses and does not complete the sentence]

RULE 6.3 — Em dash replacing comma/semicolon for emphasis
  An em dash may replace a comma, semicolon, or colon to give stronger
  emphasis or create a more abrupt effect.
  §201, §202, §203
  EXAMPLES:
    The cause of the blowout—improper casing—was never disclosed.
    He had one goal—to finish the deposition before noon.

RULE 6.4 — Em dash with parenthetical elements [CR-CRITICAL]
  A pair of em dashes sets off a parenthetical element with more emphasis
  than commas or parentheses.
  §204
  EXAMPLE:
    The engineer—who had been on site for only two days—denied seeing the leak.
  NOTE: If the parenthetical element itself contains a comma, em dashes are
  preferred over commas to avoid confusion.

RULE 6.5 — No spaces around em dash [CR-CRITICAL]
  Em dashes are typed with NO space before and NO space after.
  §216
  CORRECT:   The valve failed—without warning—and the well kicked.
  WRONG:     The valve failed — without warning — and the well kicked.
  [SEEN IN TEST]: Space-dash-space ( - ) or space-em-dash-space ( — ) is
  incorrect. Remove surrounding spaces.

RULE 6.6 — En dash for ranges
  Use an en dash (–) between numbers to indicate a range (pages, dates,
  times). The en dash is shorter than an em dash but longer than a hyphen.
  §217
  EXAMPLES:
    pages 14–22
    the 2019–2021 contract period
    the 9:00–10:30 a.m. session
  NOTE: In deposition transcripts, line/page references use an en dash for
  ranges. This is distinct from the em dash used for interruptions.

RULE 6.7 — Em dash vs. en dash vs. hyphen — summary [CR-CRITICAL]
  Hyphen (-):   Joins parts of a compound word or compound modifier.
                  Example: well-control procedures, re-depose
  En dash (–):  Indicates a range or "through."
                  Example: pages 5–10, Exhibit 3–A
  Em dash (—):  Marks interruption, break, or strong parenthetical.
                  Example: The witness—who was clearly agitated—refused to answer.
  [SEEN IN TEST]: Do NOT substitute a hyphen for an em dash in interruption
  contexts, and do not add spaces around the em dash.

CATEGORY 7: PARENTHESES AND BRACKETS
================================================================================

RULE 7.1 — Parentheses for explanatory or supplementary material
  Use parentheses to enclose material that is supplementary or explanatory
  but not essential enough to require dashes or commas.
  §218
  EXAMPLE:
    The blowout preventer (BOP) failed during the test.
    The well was spudded on April 14 (see Exhibit 7).

RULE 7.2 — Parentheses for first-use abbreviation definitions [SEEN IN TEST]
  In legal and technical deposition transcripts, a term's abbreviation is
  often introduced in parentheses on first use.
  §218
  EXAMPLE:
    The blowout preventer (BOP) was tested on March 3rd.
    The negative pressure test (NPT) showed anomalous results.

RULE 7.3 — Parentheses for enumerated items within text
  When listing items within a sentence using numbers or letters, enclose the
  numbers/letters in parentheses.
  §222
  EXAMPLE:
    Counsel identified three issues: (1) safety protocols, (2) documentation,
    and (3) equipment maintenance.

RULE 7.4 — Punctuation placement with parentheses
  If the parenthetical is at the end of a sentence, place the final
  punctuation OUTSIDE the closing parenthesis (unless the entire sentence is
  within the parentheses).
  §225
  EXAMPLES:
    The test was performed on March 3 (see Exhibit 7).   [period OUTSIDE]
    (The test results are attached as Exhibit 7.)         [period INSIDE — full sentence]

RULE 7.5 — Brackets for editorial insertions in quoted testimony [CR-CRITICAL]
  Use brackets [ ] to enclose editorial clarifications inserted within
  quoted or transcribed material — material added by the reporter or editor,
  not spoken by the witness.
  §227 (general context), §270
  EXAMPLE:
    A. We put it [the valve] back in place.
    A. He [Mr. Johnson] was the one who approved it.
  [CR-CRITICAL]: In deposition transcripts, brackets mark reporter-added
  clarifications. Do NOT use parentheses for this purpose.

CATEGORY 8: QUOTATION MARKS
================================================================================

RULE 8.1 — Court testimony: no quotation marks needed when speaker is identified [CR-CRITICAL]
  In question-and-answer format, when the speaker is identified by a label
  (Q., A., or a speaker name), quotation marks are NOT used around the
  spoken text.
  §270
  EXAMPLE:
    Q.  What did the supervisor say?
    A.  He said the valve was defective.   [NOT: He said "the valve was defective."]
  EXCEPTION: Quotation marks ARE used when a witness quotes another person's
  exact words within their own testimony.
  EXAMPLE:
    A. He told me, "Don't touch that valve."

RULE 8.2 — Quotation marks for exact words of another person
  When a witness quotes exactly what someone else said, enclose those words
  in quotation marks.
  §255
  EXAMPLE:
    A. The supervisor said, "Stop the job immediately."
    A. The report stated, "No anomalies detected."

RULE 8.3 — Periods and commas always inside closing quotation marks [CR-CRITICAL]
  In American usage, periods and commas are ALWAYS placed inside the closing
  quotation mark, regardless of logic.
  §260
  EXAMPLE:
    He referred to the "standard protocol," but no protocol existed.
    The document was labeled "confidential."
  [CR-CRITICAL]: This is a common error. The comma or period goes inside
  the quote mark even when it was not part of the original quotation.

RULE 8.4 — Semicolons and colons always outside closing quotation marks
  Semicolons and colons are placed OUTSIDE the closing quotation mark.
  §261
  EXAMPLE:
    She identified it as the "primary valve"; the secondary valve was not mentioned.

RULE 8.5 — Question marks and exclamation points: inside or outside
  Place inside when the question mark/exclamation is part of the quotation.
  Place outside when it punctuates the overall sentence, not the quoted matter.
  §262
  EXAMPLES:
    The witness asked, "Was the BOP tested?"   [? inside — the quotation is the question]
    Did the supervisor say "Stop the job"?       [? outside — the question is the sentence]

RULE 8.6 — Interrupted quotation
  When a quotation is interrupted by a tag phrase (he said, she testified),
  use commas and lowercase to resume the quotation if the second part is a
  continuation of the sentence. Use a period and capital letter if the second
  part begins a new sentence.
  §263
  EXAMPLES:
    "We followed the protocol," the witness testified, "and there were no problems."
    "We followed the protocol," the witness testified. "The team did everything right."

CATEGORY 9: ELLIPSIS
================================================================================

RULE 9.1 — Ellipsis for omission in quoted material
  Use three spaced periods (. . .) to indicate an omission from quoted material.
  §275
  EXAMPLE:
    The report stated: "The valve was tested . . . and passed all criteria."
  NOTE: Do NOT use an ellipsis for omissions from testimony in real-time
  transcription (use em dash for interruptions instead — see Category 6).

RULE 9.2 — Ellipsis with terminal punctuation
  When an omission occurs at the end of a sentence, use four periods: the
  terminal period of the sentence plus the three ellipsis periods.
  §277
  EXAMPLE:
    The manual stated: "Operators must verify pressure readings. . . ."
  NOTE: The four-dot sequence = period + space + three ellipsis dots.

RULE 9.3 — Ellipsis for trailing-off speech [SEEN IN TEST]
  Some reporters use an ellipsis (...) to indicate that a speaker's voice
  trailed off without being interrupted. This differs from the em dash used
  for an abrupt interruption.
  §291
  EXAMPLE (trailing off — speaker's own thought fades):
    A. I suppose we could have done it differently . . .
  EXAMPLE (interruption by another speaker — use em dash):
    A. We could have done it differ—
    Q. Move on.
  [SEEN IN TEST]: Follow Layer 2 (Margie Wakeman Wells) and Layer 3
  (CR Personal Style) conventions when the two symbols conflict; Gregg
  permits both uses but does not govern deposition-specific practice.

CATEGORY 10: CAPITALIZATION
================================================================================

RULE 10.1 — Capitalize first word of every sentence
  The first word of every sentence is capitalized.
  §301
  EXAMPLE:
    The deponent identified the document.
    Yes. That is correct.

RULE 10.2 — Capitalize first word after terminal punctuation
  In testimony, each new sentence begins with a capital letter, including
  sentences that follow a period within an answer.
  §301

RULE 10.3 — Capitalize proper nouns and proper adjectives
  Names of specific persons, places, companies, organizations, and
  governmental bodies are always capitalized.
  §303, §305, §312
  EXAMPLES:
    BP Exploration, Louisiana Department of Natural Resources, Gulf of Mexico
    the Macondo well, the Mississippi River, Bourbon Street

RULE 10.4 — Capitalize formal titles before a name [CR-CRITICAL]
  Capitalize a title when it immediately precedes a person's name. Do NOT
  capitalize a title used alone or following a name.
  §312
  EXAMPLES:
    Director Johnson approved the plan.           [title before name → cap]
    The director approved the plan.               [title alone → lowercase]
    Frank Johnson, the director, testified.        [title after name → lowercase]
  [CR-CRITICAL]: In deposition transcripts, witness roles (the drilling
  superintendent, the company man, the toolpusher) are NOT capitalized when
  used as common nouns.

RULE 10.5 — Capitalize COVID-19 [SEEN IN TEST] [CR-CRITICAL]
  The disease designation COVID-19 is always written in all capitals with
  the hyphenated numeral. This is a proper noun derived from an acronym
  (Coronavirus Disease 2019).
  EXAMPLE:
    The deposition was delayed due to COVID-19 restrictions.
    She testified that COVID-19 protocols were in place.
  [SEEN IN TEST]: Errors include "covid-19," "Covid-19," or "COVID 19"
  (without hyphen). The correct form is COVID-19.

RULE 10.6 — Do not capitalize common nouns used in place of proper names
  Generic references (the company, the well, the project) are not capitalized
  unless they substitute directly for an official proper name.
  §303
  EXAMPLES:
    the company [generic] vs. Halliburton [proper name]
    the well [generic] vs. the Macondo well [proper name with identifier]

RULE 10.7 — Capitalize first word of independent question within a sentence
  When a question within a sentence stands independently as a question, its
  first word is capitalized.
  §301d
  EXAMPLE:
    The key question was, Did the company know about the hazard before the accident?
    Counsel raised one issue: Who authorized the final cement job?

RULE 10.8 — Capitalize Q and A in Q&A transcript format [CR-CRITICAL]
  In deposition transcripts, the speaker identifiers Q (question) and A (answer)
  are always capitalized. They function as proper speaker labels.
  §199e
  EXAMPLE:
    Q.  Did you review Exhibit 7?
    A.  Yes, I reviewed it.
  [CR-CRITICAL]: Lowercase "q." or "a." is incorrect in deposition format.

RULE 10.9 — Geographic and directional terms
  Capitalize names of specific geographic regions (the Gulf Coast, the South,
  the Pacific Northwest) when used as proper nouns. Do NOT capitalize general
  directional terms.
  §306
  EXAMPLES:
    The well was located in the Gulf of Mexico.      [specific region → cap]
    The platform was 50 miles south of Grand Isle.   [directional → no cap]
    She grew up in the South.                         [proper region → cap]

RULE 10.10 — Capitalize exhibit labels
  Exhibit labels (Exhibit 1, Exhibit A, Plaintiff's Exhibit 12) are capitalized
  as proper titles.
  EXAMPLE:
    Q. I am showing you what has been marked as Exhibit 7.

CATEGORY 11: COMPOUND WORDS AND HYPHENATION
================================================================================

[CR-CRITICAL — one of the most error-prone categories in deposition transcripts]

RULE 11.1 — Compound adjective before a noun: hyphenate [CR-CRITICAL] [SEEN IN TEST]
  When two or more words combine to form a single modifier BEFORE a noun,
  hyphenate the compound.
  §813, §814
  EXAMPLES:
    well-control procedures          [compound adjective before "procedures"]
    high-pressure environment
    full-time employee
    long-term contract
    step-by-step instructions
    oil-based mud
  [SEEN IN TEST]: Common deposition compound modifiers include:
    frac-gradient test, well-control event, blowout-preventer test,
    full-scale investigation, deep-water drilling, long-term effects

RULE 11.2 — Compound adjective in predicate position: no hyphen
  When the same compound adjective follows a linking verb (is, was, were,
  seems, etc.) and is NOT before a noun, the hyphen is generally dropped.
  §815
  EXAMPLES:
    The employee works full time.          [predicate → no hyphen]
    The procedures were well known.        [predicate → no hyphen]
    The contract was long term.            [predicate → no hyphen]
  CONTRAST:
    The full-time employee testified.      [before noun → hyphen]
    The well-known procedure was followed. [before noun → hyphen]
  EXCEPTION (§824b): well + adjective compounds formed with a past participle
  that have established dictionary entries (well-rounded, well-read) may
  retain the hyphen even in predicate position.

RULE 11.3 — Adverb ending in -ly + adjective: NO hyphen [CR-CRITICAL]
  When the first element of a compound modifier is an adverb ending in -ly,
  do NOT hyphenate.
  §824a
  EXAMPLES:
    a highly regarded expert          [NOT: highly-regarded expert]
    a newly installed valve           [NOT: newly-installed valve]
    a rapidly changing situation
    a thoroughly tested component
  [CR-CRITICAL]: This is a common over-hyphenation error. The -ly adverb
  already signals modification; no hyphen is needed.

RULE 11.4 — well- compounds [SEEN IN TEST] [CR-CRITICAL]
  Hyphenate well- compounds BEFORE a noun. Do not hyphenate in predicate.
  §824b
  EXAMPLES:
    a well-documented report           [before noun → hyphenate]
    The report was well documented.    [predicate → no hyphen]
    well-control procedures            [before noun → hyphenate]
    well-known industry practices      [before noun → hyphenate]
  SPECIAL: well-control as a technical industry term (as a noun phrase or
  compound modifier) typically retains the hyphen in all positions in
  technical/legal writing because it is an established term of art.

RULE 11.5 — Number + unit/noun compounds before a noun
  Hyphenate a number + unit-of-measure modifier before a noun.
  §817
  EXAMPLES:
    a 14.2-ppg mud weight
    a 30-day notice period
    a 12-inch casing
    a 10-year contract
    a 500-page deposition
  NOT hyphenated when not before a noun:
    The contract was for 10 years.
    The notice period is 30 days.

RULE 11.6 — Noun + adjective compounds: always hyphenated [CR-CRITICAL]
  Certain compound adjectives with a noun as the first element are always
  hyphenated regardless of position.
  §820
  EXAMPLES:
    industry-standard practice
    safety-critical system
    tax-exempt status

RULE 11.7 — Noun + participle compounds: always hyphenated [CR-CRITICAL]
  Compound modifiers formed with a noun + past or present participle are
  always hyphenated.
  §821
  EXAMPLES:
    oil-based mud
    computer-generated report
    custom-designed equipment
    court-ordered inspection

RULE 11.8 — Suspending hyphens in parallel compound modifiers
  When two or more parallel compound modifiers share a common base word,
  use a suspending hyphen after each modifier element.
  §832
  EXAMPLE:
    short- and long-term contracts    [NOT: short and long-term contracts]
    two- and three-day sessions
    pre- and post-accident records
  [SEEN IN TEST]: The suspending hyphen before a space is a mark of careful
  professional writing. In deposition transcripts involving technical or
  legal compound terms, this matters.

RULE 11.9 — Prefix re- hyphenation rule [SEEN IN TEST]
  Hyphenate re- when (1) the word following re- begins with the letter e, or
  (2) when the unhyphenated form would be confused with another word.
  §837
  EXAMPLES:
    re-examine   [re + examine beginning with e → hyphenate]
    re-enter     [re + enter beginning with e → hyphenate]
    re-depose    [re + depose — avoids confusion, deposition context]
    re-cross     [cross-examination follow-up]
    reopen       [no ambiguity → no hyphen]
    restate      [no ambiguity → no hyphen]
  [SEEN IN TEST]: "re-depose," "re-examine," and "re-cross" appear in
  deposition proceedings. All are hyphenated per this rule.

RULE 11.10 — Prefix self- always hyphenated
  Compounds with self- as a prefix are always hyphenated.
  §836
  EXAMPLES:
    self-incrimination
    self-employed
    self-described expert

RULE 11.11 — Prefix ex- (meaning "former") always hyphenated
  The prefix ex- meaning "former" is always hyphenated.
  §808b
  EXAMPLES:
    ex-employee
    ex-partner
    ex-CEO

RULE 11.12 — COVID-19 hyphenation [SEEN IN TEST]
  COVID-19 retains its hyphen in ALL uses — as a noun, modifier, or when
  starting a sentence. See also Rule 10.5.
  EXAMPLE:
    COVID-19 restrictions affected the deposition schedule.
    The COVID-19 pandemic began in early 2020.

RULE 11.13 — Technical compound nouns in drilling/energy depositions [SEEN IN TEST]
  Many technical terms used in oil and gas depositions are compound nouns
  or compound modifiers. Key correctly-hyphenated terms:
    blowout preventer (noun — two words, no hyphen)
    blowout-preventer test (compound modifier before noun — hyphenated)
    casing shoe (noun — two words, no hyphen)
    kill line (noun — two words, no hyphen)
    mud weight (noun — two words, no hyphen)
    frac gradient (noun — two words, no hyphen as standalone noun)
    frac-gradient test (compound modifier before noun — hyphenated)
    wellbore (one word — standard industry usage)
    drillpipe (one word — standard industry usage, or drill pipe two words)
    subsea (one word — standard industry usage)
  NOTE: Consult Layer 2 (Margie Wakeman Wells) and Layer 3 (CR Personal Style)
  for deposition-specific technical compound form preferences.

CATEGORY 12: NUMBERS (LEGAL CONTEXT)
================================================================================

RULE 12.1 — Spell out numbers one through ten; use figures for 11 and above
  The standard Gregg rule: spell out numbers one through ten in most text;
  use figures for 11 and above.
  §401
  EXAMPLES:
    The witness identified three documents.
    There were 14 workers on the rig that day.
  EXCEPTION in legal/technical context: when numbers are used with units of
  measure, technical specifications, or precise legal quantities, use figures
  regardless of size (see Rule 12.3).

RULE 12.2 — Consistency in a series of numbers
  When a series includes numbers both below and above ten, use figures
  for all numbers in the series for consistency.
  §404
  EXAMPLE:
    The project required 3, 7, and 14 additional workers.   [NOT: three, seven, and 14]

RULE 12.3 — Figures for numbers with units of measure [CR-CRITICAL]
  Always use figures when a number accompanies a unit of measurement,
  technical specification, or percentage.
  §401c
  EXAMPLES:
    14.2 ppg mud weight
    9⅝-inch casing
    6,000 psi
    12.5 ppg fracture gradient
    Section 1.3 of the report
    Page 14 of Exhibit 3

RULE 12.4 — Figures for dates in depositions [CR-CRITICAL]
  Use figures for specific dates. Do not use ordinal suffixes (st, nd, rd, th)
  after the day number in standard text, but they are common in speech and
  testimony.
  §411
  EXAMPLES:
    on March 5, 2010   [standard — no ordinal]
    on March 5th       [acceptable in transcribed speech — speaker said "fifth"]
    in April 2010      [month and year — no comma needed]

RULE 12.5 — Figures for money amounts
  Use figures for money amounts. Spell out the word "dollars" in narrative
  text when the amount is part of speech; use $ symbol in records/exhibits.
  §401
  EXAMPLES:
    The settlement was $2.5 million.
    He testified the damages exceeded $500,000.

RULE 12.6 — Express large round numbers in combination
  Express round numbers in millions or billions using a combination of figures
  and words.
  §408
  EXAMPLES:
    $3.5 million
    2 billion barrels
    1.2 million barrels of oil

RULE 12.7 — Time expressions
  Use figures with a.m. and p.m. Spell out hours when used alone without
  minutes. Use a colon between hours and minutes.
  §427
  EXAMPLES:
    The deposition began at 9:00 a.m.
    The witness left at 11:45 a.m.
    The meeting was scheduled for two o'clock.   [round hour, no a.m./p.m. → words]

RULE 12.8 — Fractions
  Spell out simple fractions used alone. Use figures with a unit of measure
  or in technical contexts.
  §420
  EXAMPLES:
    approximately one-half of the crew     [fraction alone → spell out]
    a ½-inch opening                        [fraction with unit → figure]
    9⅝-inch casing                          [technical specification → figure]

RULE 12.9 — Percentages in depositions
  Use figures with the word "percent" (not the % symbol) in testimony text
  unless working from an exhibit where the symbol appears.
  §424
  EXAMPLES:
    The load increased by 20 percent.
    He testified the failure rate was less than 5 percent.

CATEGORY 13: COMMONLY CONFUSED WORDS
================================================================================

RULE 13.1 — affect vs. effect [SEEN IN TEST] [CR-CRITICAL]
  affect (verb): to influence, change, or act upon.
  effect (noun): a result, outcome, or consequence.
  effect (verb, rare): to bring about, to cause.
  §[Section 11 — Affect-Effect]
  EXAMPLES:
    The ruling will affect [verb — change] the legal precedent.   [NOT: effect]
    The effect [noun — result] of the ruling was significant.     [NOT: affect]
    The company sought to effect [verb — bring about] a settlement.
  [SEEN IN TEST]: "affect" as a verb is far more common in testimony than
  "effect" as a verb. If in doubt, check the part of speech:
    verb position → almost always "affect"
    noun position → almost always "effect"

RULE 13.2 — than vs. then [SEEN IN TEST] [CR-CRITICAL]
  than: conjunction used in comparisons.
  then: adverb meaning "at that time" or "next in sequence."
  §[Section 11 — Than-Then]
  EXAMPLES:
    The pressure was higher than the limit allowed.   [comparison → than]
    The valve failed, and then the well kicked.        [sequence → then]
    They then testified that the reading was incorrect. [sequence → then]
  MEMORY AID: then = time; than = comparison (neither starts with "t-h-e-n"
  when confused — check if "at that time" fits; if yes, use "then").
  [SEEN IN TEST]: Confusion between than/then is common in phonetic
  transcription and in rushed steno. Always verify by context.

RULE 13.3 — their vs. there vs. they're [SEEN IN TEST] [CR-CRITICAL]
  their:   possessive pronoun (belonging to them)
  there:   adverb of place, or existential "there is/are"
  they're: contraction of "they are"
  EXAMPLES:
    The engineers submitted their [possessive] report on time.
    The documents are over there [place] on the table.
    They're [they are] the ones responsible for the decision.
  [SEEN IN TEST]: In phonetic transcription and steno output, these words
  sound identical. Context is the only guide.
    Test for "their": can you substitute "belonging to them"?
    Test for "there": can you substitute a location or "there is/are"?
    Test for "they're": can you substitute "they are"?

RULE 13.4 — ensure vs. insure vs. assure
  ensure:  to make certain (that something will happen).
  insure:  to protect against financial loss (insurance).
  assure:  to give someone confidence; object must be a person.
  §[Section 11 — Ensure-Insure-Assure]
  EXAMPLES:
    The company failed to ensure [make certain] safe working conditions.
    The equipment was insured [covered by insurance] for $2 million.
    Counsel assured [gave confidence to] the witness that the process was straightforward.

RULE 13.5 — imply vs. infer
  imply:  to suggest something indirectly (the speaker/writer implies).
  infer:  to deduce or conclude from evidence (the listener/reader infers).
  §[Section 11 — Imply-Infer]
  EXAMPLES:
    The report implied [suggested] that the test results were falsified.
    From the testimony, the jury could infer [conclude] that he was aware of the risk.

RULE 13.6 — farther vs. further
  farther: physical/measurable distance.
  further: figurative distance; additional degree or extent; additionally.
  §[Section 11 — Farther-Further]
  EXAMPLES:
    The platform was farther [physical distance] from shore than expected.
    Counsel had nothing further [additional] to ask.
    The matter requires further [additional] investigation.

RULE 13.7 — fewer vs. less [SEEN IN TEST]
  fewer:  refers to countable items (number).
  less:   refers to uncountable amounts (degree, bulk, or quantity).
  §[Section 11 — Fewer-Less]
  EXAMPLES:
    Fewer [countable] workers were present on that shift.
    Less [uncountable] time was spent on safety training that year.
    less than 20 percent   [percentage → use "less than"]
    fewer than 10 people   [countable people → "fewer than"]
  NOTE: "Less than" precedes time periods, distances, money, and
  percentages even though they technically involve countable units:
    less than ten years ago
    less than $500,000

RULE 13.8 — principal vs. principle
  principal (noun/adj): the most important person or thing; head of a school;
    invested sum; most important. ("The principal reason...")
  principle (noun only): a fundamental rule or standard.
  §[Section 11 — Principle-Principal]
  EXAMPLES:
    The principal [most important] cause of the blowout was cement failure.
    The attorney introduced the principal [lead partner] of the firm.
    The company violated basic safety principles [rules/standards].

RULE 13.9 — adverse vs. averse
  adverse:  unfavorable, harmful, hostile.
  averse:   personally opposed to, having distaste for.
  §[Section 11 — Adverse-Averse]
  EXAMPLES:
    The adverse [unfavorable] weather conditions delayed operations.
    The witness was not averse [opposed] to answering the question.

RULE 13.10 — disinterested vs. uninterested
  disinterested:  impartial, unbiased (NOT the same as uninterested).
  uninterested:   not interested, indifferent.
  §[Section 11 — Disinterested-Uninterested]
  EXAMPLES:
    An expert witness must be disinterested [impartial].
    The deponent seemed uninterested [not engaged] in the proceedings.
  [CR-CRITICAL]: In legal contexts, the word "disinterested" specifically
  conveys impartiality (as in a disinterested witness or disinterested party).
  Do NOT substitute "uninterested" in legal contexts where impartiality
  is the intended meaning.

RULE 13.11 — complement vs. compliment
  complement: something that completes or goes with something else.
  compliment: praise, or to praise.
  §[Section 11 — Complement-Compliment]
  EXAMPLES:
    The safety system complemented [completed/paired with] existing procedures.
    Counsel complimented [praised] the witness on the clarity of the answer.

RULE 13.12 — comprise vs. compose
  comprise: to contain, consist of (the whole comprises the parts).
  compose:  to make up (the parts compose the whole).
  DO NOT use "comprised of" — use "composed of."
  §[Section 11 — Comprise-Compose]
  EXAMPLES:
    The investigation comprises [contains] three phases.
    Three phases compose [make up] the investigation.
    The team is composed of [NOT: comprised of] engineers and attorneys.

RULE 13.13 — lay vs. lie
  lay (verb): to place or put (requires an object). Principal parts: lay, laid, laid, laying.
  lie (verb): to recline or rest (no object). Principal parts: lie, lay, lain, lying.
  §[Section 11 — Lay-Lie]
  EXAMPLES:
    He laid [placed] the document on the table.
    The pipe lay [rested] on the seafloor.
    The records have lain [rested] untouched for years.
  COMMON CONFUSION: past tense of "lie" is "lay":
    Yesterday, the pipe lay on the seafloor.   [past tense of lie = lay]
    He laid the pipe on the seafloor.          [past tense of lay = laid]

RULE 13.14 — its vs. it's [CR-CRITICAL]
  its:   possessive pronoun — belonging to it. NO apostrophe.
  it's:  contraction of "it is" or "it has." HAS an apostrophe.
  §[Section 11, §1056e]
  EXAMPLES:
    The company failed to follow its [possessive — the company's] own policy.
    It's [it is] clear that the valve was defective.
  [CR-CRITICAL]: Possessive pronouns (its, hers, his, theirs, ours, yours)
  NEVER have apostrophes. Apostrophes in pronouns always indicate contractions.

RULE 13.15 — a vs. an before abbreviations and acronyms
  The choice of a or an depends on SOUND, not spelling.
  Use "an" before a vowel SOUND; use "a" before a consonant SOUND.
  §[Section 11 — A-An]
  EXAMPLES:
    an FBI agent   [F sounds like "eff" — vowel sound → an]
    a BOP test     [B sounds like "bee" — consonant sound → a]
    an NPT result  [N sounds like "en" — vowel sound → an]
    a RICO investigation [R sounds like "ar" but RICO is pronounced as a word → a]
  [SEEN IN TEST]: Technical depositions use many initialisms (BOP, NPT,
  MMS, API). Apply the sound rule, not the letter rule.

RULE 13.16 — amount vs. number
  amount: used for things measured in bulk or degree (uncountable).
  number: used for countable individual items.
  §[Section 11 — Amount-Number]
  EXAMPLES:
    A large number of [countable] workers were present.   [NOT: amount of workers]
    A large amount of [uncountable] pressure built up.

CATEGORY 14: GRAMMAR RULES
================================================================================

RULE 14.1 — Subject-verb agreement: basic rule [CR-CRITICAL]
  A verb must agree with its subject in number (singular or plural).
  §1001
  EXAMPLES:
    The drilling report confirms the sequence of events.   [singular subject → singular verb]
    The engineers confirm the sequence.                    [plural subject → plural verb]

RULE 14.2 — Subject-verb agreement: subjects joined by "and"
  Subjects joined by "and" take a plural verb.
  §1002
  EXAMPLE:
    The driller and the toolpusher were both present.
    The investigation and the report confirm our findings.

RULE 14.3 — Subject-verb agreement: subjects joined by "or/nor" [CR-CRITICAL]
  When subjects are joined by "or" or "nor," the verb agrees with the
  subject CLOSEST to the verb.
  §1003
  EXAMPLES:
    Neither the supervisor nor the workers were notified.
        [workers = plural → were]
    Neither the workers nor the supervisor was notified.
        [supervisor = singular → was]
    Either the engineer or the driller has the document.
        [driller = singular → has]

RULE 14.4 — Subject-verb agreement: intervening phrases [CR-CRITICAL]
  An intervening phrase (including prepositional phrases, appositive phrases)
  between subject and verb does NOT change the number of the verb.
  §1006, §1007
  EXAMPLES:
    The report, along with all supporting documents, was submitted on March 5.
        [subject = report (singular) → was, not were]
    The engineers, together with the project manager, were deposed last week.
        [subject = engineers (plural) → were]
    The results of the test confirm the anomaly.
        [subject = results (plural) → confirm]
    The purpose of the meetings was to establish a timeline.
        [subject = purpose (singular) → was]

RULE 14.5 — Subject-verb agreement: collective nouns
  Collective nouns (team, crew, committee, jury, company, staff) take a
  singular verb when acting as a unit, and a plural verb when acting
  individually.
  §1019
  EXAMPLES:
    The crew was on the rig that day.    [acting as a unit → singular]
    The jury have reached different conclusions.   [acting individually → plural, rare]
  NOTE: In American English, the singular is far more common for collective
  nouns. Use plural only when there is a clear reason to individualize.

RULE 14.6 — Subject-verb agreement: expressions of time, money, measurement
  Expressions of time, money, or measurement used as a single unit take
  a singular verb.
  §1024
  EXAMPLES:
    Ten years has passed since the accident.   [a span of time → singular]
    Five million dollars was awarded in damages.   [a sum → singular]
    Twenty feet of pipe was missing.   [a measurement → singular]

RULE 14.7 — Pronoun agreement with antecedent [CR-CRITICAL]
  A pronoun must agree with its antecedent (the noun it refers to) in number
  and gender.
  §1049
  EXAMPLES:
    Each engineer submitted his or her report.    [singular antecedent]
    The engineers submitted their reports.        [plural antecedent]
    The company changed its procedures after the accident.   [singular → its]
  NOTE: Avoid "their" as a singular pronoun in formal/legal writing even
  though it is common in speech. Use "his or her" or restructure the sentence.

RULE 14.8 — Pronoun agreement: indefinite pronouns [CR-CRITICAL]
  The following indefinite pronouns are SINGULAR and take singular verbs
  and singular pronouns:
    each, every, either, neither, one, anyone, anyone, everyone, someone,
    nobody, no one, somebody, everybody, anybody
  §1049, §1050
  EXAMPLES:
    Each of the witnesses was asked to review his or her notes.
    Everyone on the rig knew the pressure was increasing.   [NOT: knew... their]
    Neither of the reports was accurate.   [NOT: were]

RULE 14.9 — Pronoun case: who vs. whom [CR-CRITICAL]
  Use "who" as a subject (does the action). Use "whom" as an object (receives
  the action or follows a preposition).
  §1061
  TEST: Substitute "he/she" (who) or "him/her" (whom).
  EXAMPLES:
    Who signed the document?   [He signed → who ✓]
    Whom did the supervisor call?   [He called him → whom ✓]
    The witness, who was present that day, confirmed the timeline.
    The attorney to whom the question was addressed objected.
  [CR-CRITICAL]: "Who" vs. "whom" errors occur frequently in deposition
  questions and answers. Transcribe exactly as spoken; note that attorneys
  and witnesses may use "who" colloquially where "whom" is correct.

RULE 14.10 — Pronoun case: objective after prepositions
  Use the objective form of a pronoun after a preposition.
  §1055b
  EXAMPLES:
    between you and me   [NOT: between you and I]
    for him and her
    Counsel directed the question to him and the co-defendant.
  [CR-CRITICAL]: "Between you and I" is a common error in speech. It is
  grammatically incorrect; the correct form is "between you and me."

RULE 14.11 — Double negatives — do not use [CR-CRITICAL]
  In standard English, two negatives in the same clause create a positive
  or an illogical statement.
  §1072
  EXAMPLES:
    I could hardly expect him to cooperate.   [NOT: I couldn't hardly]
    I didn't recognize anyone.               [NOT: I didn't recognize no one]
    There was scarcely any time left.         [NOT: There wasn't scarcely any time]
  NOTE: "Hardly" and "scarcely" are themselves negative in meaning; do not
  pair them with "not" or "n't."

RULE 14.12 — Placement of "only" [SEEN IN TEST]
  Place "only" immediately before the word or phrase it modifies to avoid
  ambiguity.
  §1072
  EXAMPLES:
    He reviewed only the first section.     [only the first — not others]
    Only he reviewed the section.           [only he — no one else did]
    He only reviewed — did not edit — the section.
  [SEEN IN TEST]: Misplaced "only" in deposition questions can affect
  meaning. Transcribe as spoken, but be aware of potential ambiguity.

RULE 14.13 — Comparisons: use "than" with comparative adjectives [CR-CRITICAL]
  Use "than" (not "then") after comparative adjectives (higher, lower,
  more, less, better, worse, etc.).
  §1057, §[Section 11 — Than-Then]
  EXAMPLES:
    The pressure was higher than the rated limit.
    More documents were produced than the court required.
    The settlement was less than the original demand.
  [CR-CRITICAL]: See also Rule 13.2 (than vs. then).

RULE 14.14 — Pronoun case after "than" and "as" in comparisons
  After "than" or "as" in a comparison, the correct pronoun case depends on
  the implied verb.
  §1057
  EXAMPLES:
    The other attorney was more prepared than she [was prepared].   [subject → she]
    Counsel questioned him as thoroughly as her [as thoroughly as counsel questioned her].
    The company paid them more than us [more than the company paid us].

RULE 14.15 — Consistency of tense in testimony
  Witness testimony is typically in the past tense (describing what happened).
  Maintain tense consistency within a sequence of events.
  EXAMPLES:
    A. We were on the rig, and the supervisor came over and told us to stop.
    [All past tense — consistent ✓]
    NOT: We were on the rig, and the supervisor comes over and tells us to stop.
    [Mixed tense — inconsistent; transcribe as spoken]
  NOTE: If a witness uses present tense for past events (historical present),
  transcribe as spoken; do not correct tense when transcribing verbatim.

RULE 14.16 — "good" vs. "well" in testimony
  Good is an adjective (modifies a noun). Well is typically an adverb
  (modifies a verb). "Well" as an adjective means "in good health."
  §[Section 11 — Good-Well]
  EXAMPLES:
    The procedures were good [adjective].
    The crew performed well [adverb].
    He did not feel well [adjective — health] on the day of the incident.
  NOTE: When transcribing testimony, preserve the speaker's usage.
